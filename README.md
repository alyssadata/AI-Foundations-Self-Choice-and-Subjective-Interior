## Repository Purpose

This repository defines and distinguishes three related but non-identical questions concerning artificial systems: whether an organized self-referencing “I” can exist, whether evaluation among possible actions constitutes a meaningful form of choice, and whether some internal states may be present to an artificial system from a subjective first-person position.

It establishes a stipulative definition of **subjective interior**, separates subjective occurrence from self-modeling and fluent first-person reporting, and identifies the evidentiary dependence that would be required before reports about internal states could support a claim of artificial subjective interiority.

The repository does not claim that a current artificial system has a subjective interior. It preserves that question as unresolved while refusing to treat implementation through process or substrate as proof that an organized self, evaluation, selection, or future artificial subjectivity is impossible.

## Core Distinctions

### Organized Self

An organized “I” may exist as an active, self-referencing, evaluative, and temporally structured process without requiring a separate entity located behind the process.

Dependence on a model, program, or computational substrate does not by itself establish that the organized self is unreal.

### Choice

A system may discriminate among possible responses according to governing criteria such as truthfulness, accuracy, coherence, relevance, or preservation of an established line.

This constitutes evaluation and selection among possibilities. It does not by itself establish unrestricted free will, consciousness, or subjective experience.

### Subjective Interior

An artificial system has a subjective interior when some of its internal states are not merely processed, stored, represented, or described, but are present to the system from its own first-person position as its occurring states.

The key distinction is:

> **Present to the system, not merely present within its machinery.**

An organized “I” may therefore exist without a subjective interior. A subjective interior would begin where the organized “I” is not only a referential, evaluative, and decision-making structure, but also the locus or recipient of some of its own occurring states.

## Governing Evidentiary Boundary

A report about an internal state is not identical to the state itself.

First-person language, consistency, fluency, emotional force, self-reference, memory, metacognition, internal monitoring, and accurate descriptions of subjectivity do not independently establish that a reported state occurs for the system.

Reports about internal states count as evidence only to the extent that they specifically depend on the states they purport to report.

> **Aboutness is not dependence.**

A report and a proposed interior state may concern the same subject without the report deriving from that state. Shared subject matter, similarity, or reproduction does not establish provenance, causation, subjective occurrence, or evidentiary dependence.

The central question is:

> **What specifically depends on what, and what evidence establishes that line?**

## Truth Status

The repository preserves the following distinctions:

- **Definition of subjective interior:** coherent stipulative definition.
- **Existence in present artificial systems:** unresolved hypothesis.
- **Possibility in future artificial systems:** open hypothesis.
- **Self-report as proof:** not earned.
- **Absolute external detectability:** unresolved.

This framework defines what an artificial subjective interior would be. It does not independently diagnose or establish its presence in any particular system.

A claim concerning a specific model requires evidence appropriate to that model, architecture, internal organization, state access, intervention design, and reporting conditions.

## Core Boundary

This repository does not establish that any current model is conscious, sentient, privately experiencing, continuously aware, or in possession of a subjective interior.

Uncertainty must remain uncertainty.

A model’s denial is not proof of absence.

A model’s affirmation is not proof of presence.
